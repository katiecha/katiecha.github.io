Anyone can develop an occupational heat-related illness (OHRI), but some populations are more at risk -- specifically immigrant workers.6

Check out the following determinants (factors) that increase your risk as an immigrant worker:



Immigrant workers (citizen and noncitizen) may face additional OHRI risks because of the United State's strict insurance and medical systems.6

For many immigrants, getting health insurance is impossible due to US laws.6

30% of noncitizen immigrant workers lack health insurance, while only 15% of US citizens lack health insurance.4

When migrant workers forgo insurance, they often forgo healthcare as well, and this can worsen OHRI symptoms and outcomes.6


Many immigrant workers are forced to work dangerous jobs like construction and farming.6

These jobs offer less benefits and protections (e.g., overtime pay, healthcare) meant to prevent OHRIs.6

This category of job also tends to be seasonal, temporary, or generally unsecure.6

The lack of security prevents immigrant workers from reporting cases of OHRI, since the workers may fear job loss or immigration consequences more than potential health risks.7


Language barriers and the confusing US healthcare system can prevent immigrant workers from getting treatment for OHRIs.6

Language issues can also prevent immigrant workers from understanding trainings that lower the risk of developing an OHRI.6

In general, the structure of the United States heathcare system -- whether due to language or insurance barriers -- prevents easy access for certain populations, worsening the outcomes of OHRI.6


Undocumented immigrant face even more challenges that can increase the risk of developing OHRIs.6

On the topic of pay, undocumented immigrants are 2x more likely to experience wage violations.6

This translates to other types of violations -- specifically safety violations.6

Fearing possible deportation, undocumented immigrant workers report even fewer cases of OHRI than immigrant workers.6

This group also has fewer workplace protections than citizen and noncitizen workers.6

Many of these issues are caused by strict US immigration laws, a national determinant that increases rates of OHRI.7


While many structural issues exist within the United States, the health inequities experienced by immigrant workers can be reduced as OHRI is very preventable.1

For example, the buddy system* can be used to decrease the number of OHRI cases in a community.

*The buddy system pairs newer and more experienced workers together to help identify symptoms of HRI in the workplace.

For more information on OHRI prevention, check out OSHA's resources and prevention tips on heat exposure.
